Current Issue

Volume 105, Issue 4: August 20, 2013

Cover picture: Kandinsky-inspired rendition of small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) of biological macromolecules. Circle elements represent the raw SAXS images that could be used to distinguish different macromolecules or conformational states. The upper left panel is the Rad50 ABC-ATPase (PDB:3QKT), and the lower right panel is the signal recognition particle (PDB:1DUL). Images were constructed from the letters SAXS using Textify.it. The figure was created by Robert Rambo, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory. See the article by Schneidman-Duhovny et al. on page 962.

Emerging Biophysical Technologies


Pulsed Interleaved Excitation Fluctuation Imaging

Jelle Hendrix, Waldemar Schrimpf, Matthias Höller, Don C. Lamb

Combining pulsed interleaved excitation with fluctuation imaging methods improves the latter by allowing crosstalk-free measurements. Most significantly, it extends the methods to lifetime measurements, which greatly expands the possible experimental applications.


Optoporation and Genetic Manipulation of Cells Using Femtosecond Laser Pulses

Andrew A. Davis, Matthew J. Farrar, Nozomi Nishimura, Moonsoo M. Jin, Chris B. Schaffer

Optoporation (creation of pores in the plasma membrane using laser light) is used in a new way: few (femtosecond) isolated pulses of high energy, as opposed to trains of (millions of) low-energy pulses, resulting in similar transfection success rates and cell viability. This new method may allow optoporation deep inside tissues, since the high pulse energy would compensate for losses due to scattering, while the thermal damage at the tissue surface would be greatly reduced.
